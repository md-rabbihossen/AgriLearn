Linkage and Crossing Over

An exploration of the fundamental genetic concepts of linkage and crossing over, their mechanisms, significance, and interplay in heredity.

linkIntroduction to Linkage

The tendency of genes to remain together in their original combination during inheritance is called linkage. This means that alleles at different loci on the same chromosome are often inherited as a single unit.

The phenomenon of linkage was first reported by William Bateson and Reginald Punnett in 1906 while studying sweet peas. They observed that certain traits did not assort independently as predicted by Mendel's laws.

Later, Thomas Hunt Morgan, through his work with Drosophila melanogaster (fruit flies), extensively studied linkage and put forth the theory of linkage. He concluded that "coupling" (alleles of the same type, e.g., dominant with dominant, on the same chromosome) and "repulsion" (alleles of different types, e.g., dominant with recessive, on the same chromosome) were two phases of the single phenomenon of linkage.

biotechChromosomal Theory of Inheritance

The Chromosomal Theory of Inheritance, proposed independently by Walter Sutton and Theodor Boveri in the early 20th century, provides the physical basis for Mendel's laws and the concept of linkage. Key tenets include:

  • Genes are located on chromosomes.
  • Chromosomes undergo segregation during meiosis, and assort independently (for chromosomes carrying different sets of genes).
  • Genes located on the same chromosome (linked genes) tend to be inherited together because the chromosome acts as a single unit of transmission during meiosis.

It is now well established that many genes are located on each chromosome in a linear fashion. All genes located on the same chromosome would typically move to the same pole during cell division. As a consequence, such genes will fail to show independent segregation and would tend to be inherited together, demonstrating linkage.

categoryTypes of Linkage

Linkage is generally classified based on three criteria:

A) Based on Crossing Over

  • Complete Linkage: Occurs when linked genes are so close together on a chromosome that crossing over (exchange of genetic material) between them is virtually absent. This results in only parental combinations of alleles in the offspring. Complete linkage is rare but has been observed, for example, in males of Drosophila and females of silkworms, where there is a complete absence of recombinant types due to the absence of crossing over for certain gene pairs.
  • Incomplete/Partial Linkage: This is more common. It occurs when linked genes are separated by some distance on the chromosome, allowing for crossing over to occur between them, albeit at a frequency less than 50%. This results in both parental and recombinant (new combinations) types of offspring. Incomplete linkage has been observed in maize, pea, Drosophila females, and several other organisms.

B) Based on Genes Involved (Phases of Linkage)

This refers to the arrangement of alleles on the homologous chromosomes:

  • Coupling Phase (cis arrangement): When dominant alleles of two or more linked genes are present on one chromosome, and their corresponding recessive alleles are on the homologous chromosome (e.g., AB/ab).
    TR / tr ---- Coupling phase
  • Repulsion Phase (trans arrangement): When a dominant allele of one gene and a recessive allele of another linked gene are present on one chromosome, and the reverse is true for the homologous chromosome (e.g., Ab/aB).
    Tr / tR ---- Repulsion phase

C) Based on Chromosomes Involved

  • Autosomal Linkage: Refers to the linkage of genes located on autosomes (non-sex chromosomes).
  • Allosomal Linkage (Sex Linkage): Refers to the linkage of genes located on sex chromosomes (e.g., X or Y chromosome, typically X-linked).

fact_checkCharacteristics of Linkage

  • Linkage involves two or more genes located on the same chromosome in a linear fashion.
  • Linkage reduces variability by keeping parental gene combinations together.
  • Linkage may involve either dominant or recessive alleles (coupling phase) or some dominant and some recessive alleles (repulsion phase).
  • Linkage usually involves genes that are located close to each other.
  • The strength of linkage is inversely proportional to the distance between the linked genes. Closer genes show stronger linkage (less recombination), while genes farther apart show weaker linkage (more recombination).
  • Linkage can be determined from test cross progeny data by observing deviations from expected Mendelian ratios.
  • Linked genes tend to remain in their original combination during the course of inheritance.

groupsLinkage Groups

A linkage group refers to all the genes located on a single chromosome. These genes are inherited together as a unit. The number of linkage groups in an organism is equal to its haploid number of chromosomes.

Examples:

  • Corn (Maize, Zea mays, 2n=20): 10 linkage groups
  • Garden Pea (Pisum sativum, 2n=14): 7 linkage groups
  • Barley (Hordeum vulgare, 2n=14): 7 linkage groups
  • Fruit Fly (Drosophila melanogaster, 2n=8): 4 linkage groups
  • Humans (Homo sapiens, 2n=46): 23 linkage groups

searchDetection of Linkage

The most common method for detecting linkage is the test cross. In this method, an F1 individual heterozygous at two (or more) loci (e.g., AB/ab) is crossed with a parent homozygous recessive for all genes being studied (e.g., ab/ab).

  • If genes are unlinked (independent assortment): The test cross progeny will show a 1:1:1:1 ratio of the four possible phenotypic classes (two parental, two recombinant).
  • If genes are linked: The frequency of parental types will be significantly higher than 50%, and the frequency of recombinant types will be significantly lower than 50%. The deviation from the 1:1:1:1 ratio indicates linkage. The percentage of recombinant offspring (recombination frequency) is used to measure the distance between linked genes.

visibilityExamples of Linkage

Linkage in Maize (Corn)

Hutchinson conducted experiments with maize, crossing a variety with coloured and full seeds (CCSS) with a variety having colourless and shrunken seeds (ccss). The gene C (colour) is dominant over c (colourless), and S (full seed) is dominant over s (shrunken seed).

The F1 generation (CcSs) all had coloured, full seeds. When F1 females were test-crossed with colourless, shrunken (ccss) males, the following results were observed (approximate percentages from PDF):

  • Coloured, full (CS - parental): ~48.2%
  • Colourless, shrunken (cs - parental): ~48.2%
  • Coloured, shrunken (Cs - recombinant): ~1.8%
  • Colourless, full (cS - recombinant): ~1.8%

Parental combinations (CS and cs) accounted for about 96.4%, while new combinations (Cs and cS) were about 3.6%. This significant deviation from the expected 1:1:1:1 ratio (if unlinked) clearly indicates that the genes for seed colour and seed shape are linked in maize. This is an example of incomplete linkage.

Linkage in Drosophila melanogaster (Fruit Fly)

T.H. Morgan's experiments with Drosophila were pivotal in understanding linkage.

Experiment 1 (White eyes, Miniature wings):

Morgan crossed female flies with white eyes (w) and miniature wings (m) (both recessive) with wild-type males (red eyes w+, normal wings m+). The F1 females were wild-type (heterozygous w+m+/wm). When these F1 females were test-crossed, the parental types (white eyes, miniature wings and wild-type eyes, wild-type wings) were far more numerous than the recombinant types (white eyes, wild-type wings and wild-type eyes, miniature wings). The recombination frequency was calculated to be about 36.8% (from PDF image data), indicating linkage.

Experiment 2 (Grey body, Long wings - Complete Linkage Example in Males):

Morgan crossed wild-type Drosophila with grey body and long wings (BBVV) with a mutant type having black body and vestigial wings (bbvv). The F1 hybrids (BbVv) were grey-bodied with long wings.

When an F1 male (BbVv) was back-crossed with a double recessive female (bbvv), only parental combinations (grey body, long wings and black body, vestigial wings) appeared in the F2 generation. No new combinations were observed. This indicated complete linkage between the genes for body colour and wing type in Drosophila males for these specific genes, meaning no crossing over occurred between them in males.

insightsSignificance of Linkage in Plant Breeding

  • Linkage limits variability among individuals by keeping parental gene combinations together.
  • Linkage between two or more loci controlling different desirable characters is advantageous for a plant breeder. It helps in the simultaneous improvement of both characters.
  • Linkage is undesirable when desirable and undesirable genes are linked together, making it difficult to separate them. This is known as "linkage drag."
  • Linkage plays an important role in determining the nature and scope of hybridization and selection programs.
  • Linkage reduces the chance of recombination of genes and thus helps to hold parental characteristics together. It helps organisms maintain their parental, racial, and other characters. For this reason, plant and animal breeders may find it difficult to combine various characters if they are trying to break unfavorable linkages.

swap_horizCrossing Over

Crossing over is the exchange of genetic material between non-sister chromatids of homologous chromosomes during meiosis (specifically, in Prophase I). This process results in new allelic combinations in the daughter cells, leading to genetic recombination.

Key points about crossing over:

  • Each diploid cell contains two copies of every chromosome (one maternal, one paternal), forming homologous chromosomes.
  • During sexual reproduction, diploid organisms produce haploid gametes through meiosis.
  • In Prophase I of meiosis, homologous chromosomes pair up (synapsis) and exchange segments of genetic material.
  • The resultant chromosomes are recombinants, containing a mixture of genes derived from both maternal and paternal chromosomes.

bug_reportExample of Crossing Over in Drosophila

Consider factors for eye color and wing shape in Drosophila: pink eyes (r) and curled wings (c) are recessive, while red eyes (R) and straight wings (C) are dominant.

If a pure-breeding fly with red eyes and straight wings (RRCC) is mated with a fly having pink eyes and curled wings (rrcc):

  • The F1 hybrids are all red-eyed and straight-winged (RrCc).

When an F1 female (RrCc) is back-crossed with a male having pink eyes and curled wings (rrcc - test cross), the F2 generation might show (example percentages from PDF):

  • Red eyes, straight wings (RC - parental): 49%
  • Pink eyes, curled wings (rc - parental): 49%
  • Red eyes, curled wings (Rc - recombinant/cross-over): 1%
  • Pink eyes, straight wings (rC - recombinant/cross-over): 1%

The parental types constitute 98%, while the recombinant (cross-over) types constitute 2%. This indicates that the genes for eye color and wing shape are linked, and crossing over occurs between them at a frequency of 2%.

If the genes were on different chromosomes (unlinked), all four classes would appear in approximately equal numbers (25% each). If they were completely linked with no crossing over, only the two parental types would appear.

psychologyTheories of Chromosomal Crossing Over

Several theories have been proposed to explain the mechanism of crossing over:

1. The 'Contact First' Theory (Serebrovsky)

This theory states that two non-sister chromatids first contact and then cross each other. Breakage occurs at the point of contact, and the broken segments reunite to form new combinations.

2. The 'Breakage First' Theory (Muller)

According to this theory, two inner non-sister chromatids first break into segments without crossing over. Then, the broken segments reunite with each other, forming new arrangements and resulting in new combinations. This theory is widely accepted.

3. Strain Theory (Precosity Theory - Darlington)

Darlington proposed that chromosomal breakage occurs due to strain or tension during pairing. When homologous chromosomes pair and twist around each other (relational coiling), tension develops on the chromatids, causing them to break at points of contact. Recombination occurs when broken ends rejoin, not necessarily with their original segments. Chiasmata (visible cross-over points) are thought to hold the broken chromatids together.

4. Belling's Hypothesis

Belling (1933) suggested that crossing over results from an exchange between newly duplicated chromatids during their formation. New chromomeres (segments of a chromosome) are formed alongside their sister chromosomes, and interconnecting fibers form later. If relational coiling exists, these fibers might form between non-sister chromomeres, producing a cross-over.

shuffleKinds of Crossing Over

Crossing over can be classified based on the number of chiasmata (points of exchange) present:

  • Single Crossing Over: Occurs when there is only one chiasma or crossing over event at one point in a chromosome pair. This produces single cross-over gametes.
  • Double Crossing Over: Occurs when crossing over happens at two points in the same chromosome pair. This produces double cross-over gametes and is less frequent than single crossing over.
  • Multiple Crossing Over: Crossing over can also occur at three, four, or more points in the same chromosome pair, referred to as triple, quadruple, or multiple crossing over, respectively. These are progressively rarer.

constructionMechanism of Crossing Over (Historical Theories)

1. Classical Theory (Morgan and Sharp - Two-Plane Theory)

This theory, now largely of historical significance, proposed that chiasma formation occurs when a chromatid of one chromosome associates with a non-sister chromatid of its homolog. It suggested chiasma formation was the cause, not the result, of crossing over, and that not every chiasma led to recombination. This theory is not supported by current evidence.

2. Chiasma Type Theory (Janssens, Belling, Darlington - Breakage & Reunion Theory / One-Plane Theory)

This widely accepted theory posits that breakage occurs in non-sister chromatids of homologous chromosomes, followed by the reunion of chromatid segments, resulting in crossing over. Chiasmata are formed at the points where crossing over has taken place when homologous chromosomes begin to separate during the diplotene stage of Prophase I.

Key points:

  • Chiasma is the result (cytological manifestation) of a crossing over event.
  • Each chiasma generally corresponds to one genetic crossing over event (1:1 ratio).

While generally supported, some observations (like chiasmata in male Drosophila where genetic crossing over is rare for some chromosomes) have led to further refinements and understanding that not all chiasmata might lead to reciprocal recombination in all cases or species.

3. Copy-Choice Theory (Lederberg)

Proposed by J. Lederberg (1955), this theory suggested that duplication and recombination occur simultaneously. Newly synthesized genes could switch templates during replication, leading to recombinant strands. This theory faces objections, such as the fact that crossing over involves existing chromatids, not just newly synthesized ones, and DNA replication largely precedes synapsis and crossing over.

hubMolecular Mechanism of Crossing Over (Modern View)

The modern understanding of crossing over involves precise molecular events:

  1. Double-Strand Break (DSB): The process often begins with a programmed double-strand break in the DNA of one chromatid, typically initiated by specific enzymes like Spo11.
  2. Resection: Exonucleases (enzymes that remove nucleotides) process the broken ends, removing short stretches of nucleotides from the 5' ends. This creates 3' single-stranded tails.
  3. Strand Invasion: One of the 3' single-stranded tails, coated with proteins like Rad51 (a recombinase), "invades" the homologous DNA duplex of a non-sister chromatid, searching for a complementary sequence.
  4. D-loop Formation: Upon finding homology, the invading strand displaces one strand of the homologous duplex, forming a displacement loop (D-loop).
  5. DNA Synthesis: The invading 3' end acts as a primer for DNA synthesis, using the intact strand of the homologous chromatid as a template. This extends the invading strand.
  6. Capture of the Second End & Holliday Junction Formation: The displaced D-loop can pair with the other 3' single-stranded tail from the original DSB. Further DNA synthesis and ligation (joining of DNA fragments by DNA ligase) can lead to the formation of a structure with two crossover points called a double Holliday junction. A Holliday junction is a branched nucleic acid structure containing four DNA strands.
  7. Resolution of Holliday Junctions: The Holliday junctions must be resolved (cut and rejoined) to separate the homologous chromosomes. This resolution can occur in two ways:
    • Crossover: If the two junctions are resolved by cutting different pairs of strands (one inner and one outer at each junction, or specific orientations), it results in an exchange of flanking chromosomal arms, leading to a genetic crossover.
    • Non-crossover: If both junctions are resolved by cutting the same pairs of strands (e.g., both inner strands), it results in gene conversion (a short segment of DNA from one chromatid is copied to the other) without the exchange of flanking arms. This is often associated with the Synthesis-Dependent Strand Annealing (SDSA) pathway.

Proteins like MSH4 and MSH5 are involved in stabilizing Holliday junctions and promoting crossover outcomes.

tuneFactors Affecting Crossing Over

The frequency of crossing over can be influenced by various genetic, physiological, and environmental factors:

  • Sex: Crossing over frequency often differs between sexes. For example, it's generally absent or greatly reduced in male Drosophila and female silkworms. The heterogametic sex (e.g., XY males in humans) often shows lower recombination rates than the homogametic sex (e.g., XX females).
  • Mutation: Certain gene mutations can affect crossing over rates. For instance, some mutations in Drosophila reduce crossing over across all chromosomes.
  • Age: The age of the individual can influence crossing over. In Drosophila females, for example, crossing over tends to decrease with age.
  • Inversion: Chromosomal inversions (where a segment of a chromosome is flipped) suppress crossing over within the inverted region in heterozygotes. However, crossing over might increase in adjacent, normally paired regions.
  • X-radiation and other Radiations: Exposure to X-rays (Muller) or radium (Hanson) can increase the frequency of crossing over.
  • Temperature: Extreme temperatures (both low and high) can increase crossing over frequency in organisms like female Drosophila (Plough).
  • Centromere Vicinity (Chromosomal Region): Crossing over is generally less frequent near centromeres (centromeric heterochromatin) and at the tips of chromosomes (telomeres). It's more frequent in euchromatic regions.
  • Somatic Crossing Over: While rare, crossing over can occur in somatic (body) cells, known as mitotic crossing over. This was first studied by Stern in Drosophila. It can lead to patches of tissue with a recombinant genotype but has no genetic importance for inheritance to offspring. Certain mutations (e.g., "minute" bristles in Drosophila) can increase its frequency.

Factors that affect crossing over will inversely affect the strength of linkage (e.g., increased crossing over leads to weaker linkage).

functionsFunctions of Crossing Over

Crossing over plays crucial roles in genetics and evolution:

  • Increases Genetic Variability: It is a primary source of genetic variation in sexually reproducing populations by creating new combinations of alleles on chromosomes.
  • Facilitates Evolution: Genetic variation is the raw material for natural selection. Crossing over allows favorable mutations that arise on different chromosomes to be combined, and unfavorable combinations to be broken apart.
  • Prevents Muller's Ratchet: In asexual populations, deleterious mutations can accumulate irreversibly (Muller's Ratchet). Sexual reproduction with crossing over allows for the creation of chromosomes with fewer deleterious mutations.
  • Ensures Proper Chromosome Segregation: In many organisms, at least one crossover per chromosome arm is necessary for the proper pairing and subsequent segregation of homologous chromosomes during Meiosis I. Chiasmata physically hold homologs together until anaphase I.
  • Allows Gene Mapping: The frequency of crossing over between linked genes is proportional to the distance between them. This principle is used to construct genetic maps, showing the relative locations of genes on chromosomes.
  • Reduces Linkage Disequilibrium: It helps to break down associations between alleles at different loci, moving populations closer to linkage equilibrium.
  • Adaptability: The genetic diversity generated by crossing over can increase a population's ability to adapt to changing environments and resist diseases. If an epidemic occurs, genetic variability reduces the chance of the entire population being wiped out.
