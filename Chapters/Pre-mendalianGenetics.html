biotechIntroduction to Genetics

The term 'Genetics' was coined by William Bateson in 1905, a proponent of Mendel's work. It is derived from the Greek word gennō, meaning "to give birth".

Genetics is the branch of biology that deals with heredity and variation. It explores the principles that explain the similarities between parents and their offspring, as well as the differences among individuals of a single species. Genetics is concerned with the transmission, expression, and evolution of genes, the fundamental molecules that control the function, development, and ultimate appearance of individuals.

For example, why does a human being give birth to a human being and not to any other organism? This fundamental question is at the heart of genetics.

family_restroomHeredity and Variation

Children resemble their parents due to heredity, yet they also differ from their parents to some extent due to variation. These two concepts are central to genetics.

Heredity

Heredity is the sum of all biological processes by which particular characteristics are transmitted from parents to their offspring. It is the potential of an individual to transmit its characters to the next generation, aiming to maintain uniformity.

Variation

Variation refers to the dissimilarities or differences that exist in the genetic makeup of individuals within a population. Variation brings in plasticity and is crucial for evolution. It can be influenced by both genetic factors and environmental interactions.

Environmental Variation: This type of variation is the alteration of an organism's phenotype (observable characteristics) in response to environmental factors. It is due to components like temperature, moisture, light, etc., and these changes are generally not inherited.

hubGene, Genotype, and Phenotype

Gene

A gene is the basic physical and functional unit of heredity. Genes are segments of DNA that are transferred from a parent to offspring and are responsible for determining some characteristic of the offspring. They contain information for a specific trait and are transcribed and translated by the cell to make proteins, which in turn create a visible phenotype.

Example: One gene might code for eye color. The cell uses this gene to make proteins that create green pigment in the eyes.

Genotype

The genotype refers to the genetic composition of an individual, consisting of its heritable genes. Genotypes are present within the chromosomes and are not directly observable.

Phenotype

The phenotype refers to all the observable traits of an organism, which result from the interaction of its genotype with the environment. Phenotypes are the physical expressions of genes, such as height, eye color, or behavior.

account_treeBranches of Genetics

Genetics is a vast field with numerous specialized branches. Some of the key branches include:

  • Microbial Genetics: Studies genetics of microorganisms like bacteria and archaea, often for genetic engineering.
  • Mycogenetics: Focuses on the general genetics of fungi.
  • Animal Genetics: Studies genes in animals, impacting performance and traits passed to offspring.
  • Human Genetics: The scientific study of inherited human variation and genetic disorders.
  • Cytogenetics: Studies chromosome structure, properties, behavior during cell division (mitosis and meiosis), and their influence on phenotype.
  • Quantitative Genetics: Deals with the genetics of complex traits (polygenic traits) that are affected by multiple genes and environmental factors (e.g., height, milk yield).
  • Population Genetics: Studies the genetic composition of biological populations and changes due to factors like natural selection.
  • Biochemical Genetics: Focuses on the biochemical principles underlying genome structure, expression, and evolution.
  • Molecular Genetics: Studies the molecular structure of DNA, its cellular activities (like replication), and its role in determining an organism's makeup.
  • Clinical Genetics: Provides diagnostic services and genetic counseling for individuals/families with or at risk of genetic conditions.
  • Developmental Genetics: Studies how genotype is transformed into phenotype during an organism's development.
  • Radiation Genetics: Investigates the genetic effects of radiation and their impact on populations.
  • Plant Genetics: Focuses on heredity in plants, crucial for crop improvement.
  • Ecological Genetics: Studies genetics in natural populations, focusing on traits involved in species interactions and adaptation to the environment.

flagLandmarks in the History of Genetics

The field of genetics has evolved through numerous groundbreaking discoveries. Here are some key milestones:

1595

Janssen: Invention of the single-lens optical microscope.

1859

Charles Darwin: Published 'On the Origin of Species by means of Natural Selection'.

1865

Gregor Mendel: Introduced the fundamental laws of inheritance. (Often called the "Father of Genetics")

Robert Hooke: Described "cells" for the first time.

1888

Waldeyer: Described "chromosomes".

1902

Boveri (and Sutton independently): Proposed the chromosome theory of inheritance, linking chromosomes to Mendelian factors.

1910

Thomas Hunt Morgan: Showed that genes are located on chromosomes, using fruit flies (Drosophila melanogaster).

1953

James Watson and Francis Crick: Described the double helix structure of DNA, based on work by Rosalind Franklin and Maurice Wilkins.

1956

Levan and Tjio: Reported the correct human chromosome number as 46.

1977

Frederick Sanger (and independently Maxam & Gilbert): Developed methods for DNA sequencing.

1985

Kary Mullis: Invented the Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR).

1990

Human Genome Project: Initiated with the goal of sequencing the entire human genome.

2001-2003

First draft (2001) and complete sequence (2003) of the human genome published.

2012-2020

CRISPR-Cas9: Development of genome editing technology by Emmanuelle Charpentier and Jennifer Doudna (Nobel Prize in Chemistry 2020).

history_eduPre-Mendelian Concepts of Heredity

Before Gregor Mendel's groundbreaking work in the mid-19th century, many theories were proposed to explain how traits are passed from parents to offspring. Most of these early ideas assumed that parental characters mixed or "blended" in the offspring. These are collectively known as theories of blending inheritance.

Early observations, such as Grew reporting reproductive organs in plants (1682) and Fairchild producing a hybrid plant (1717), hinted at the mechanisms of heredity but lacked a scientific framework.

1. Moist Vapour Theory (Pythagoras, c. 500 BC)

Pythagoras proposed that moist vapours derived from various body parts of the male (brain, nerves, etc.) were transmitted to the female during conception. These vapours were thought to coalesce in the female to form the new individual. According to this theory, the male contributed all the characters of the embryo, while the female merely provided the environment for development.

help_outlineImportant Questions for Your Exam

  • Describe Pythagoras's Moist Vapour Theory of heredity.
  • According to this theory, what was the role of the male and female parent in contributing to the offspring's characteristics?

2. Fluid Theory (Empedocles, c. 504-433 BC)

Empedocles suggested that each body part of both parents produced a specific fluid. These fluids from different body parts would mix together during reproduction to form the embryo. Any defect in the "descent and mixing up of the fluids" could result in the offspring missing characters from one or both parents.

help_outlineImportant Questions for Your Exam

  • Explain Empedocles' Fluid Theory of heredity.
  • How did this theory account for the contribution of both parents to the offspring?

3. Reproductive Blood Theory (Aristotle, c. 384-322 BC)

Aristotle believed that an embryo was formed from the mixing of "reproductive blood" from both parents. He considered the male's reproductive blood to be more "purified" (semen) and the female's to be less so (menstrual blood). As a result, he posited that the male contributed more to the characteristics of the offspring, particularly the "form," while the female contributed the "matter."

help_outlineImportant Questions for Your Exam

  • What was Aristotle's Reproductive Blood Theory?
  • How did Aristotle differentiate the contributions of male ("form") and female ("matter") parents in this theory?

4. Preformation Theory (17th-18th Century)

Proposed by biologists like Swammerdam and Bonnet. This theory stated that a miniature, fully formed human, called a homunculus, was already present in either the egg (ovists) or the sperm (spermists). Development was simply the growth of this preformed individual. It was rejected as it couldn't explain blended traits or new variations.

help_outlineImportant Questions for Your Exam

  • Describe the Preformation Theory. What is a "homunculus"?
  • What were the two main schools of thought within Preformationism (ovists vs. spermists)?
  • Why was the Preformation Theory eventually rejected by the scientific community?

5. Theory of Epigenesis (C.F. Wolff, 1738-1794)

Caspar Friedrich Wolff opposed preformation, arguing that gametes contained undifferentiated substance that gradually organized and differentiated to form the embryo de novo (from scratch), guided by "vital forces."

help_outlineImportant Questions for Your Exam

  • Explain C.F. Wolff's Theory of Epigenesis.
  • How did the Theory of Epigenesis differ fundamentally from the Preformation Theory?
  • What does "de novo" formation mean in the context of epigenesis?

6. Theory of Acquired Characters (Jean-Baptiste Lamarck, 1744-1829)

Lamarck proposed that characteristics acquired by an individual during its lifetime (e.g., strong muscles from exercise) could be passed to offspring. This was later largely disproven.

help_outlineImportant Questions for Your Exam

7. Theory of Pangenesis (Hippocrates, c. 400 BC; Charles Darwin, 1809-1882)

This theory suggested that all parts of the body produce minute particles called gemmules or pangenes, which travel to sex organs and assemble into gametes, transmitting traits from all body parts.

help_outlineImportant Questions for Your Exam

  • Describe the Theory of Pangenesis. Who were its main proponents?

8. Germplasm Theory (August Weismann, 1834-1914)

Weismann distinguished between Somatoplasm (body cells, changes not inherited) and Germplasm (reproductive cells, responsible for heredity). This theory emphasized that only changes in germplasm are inherited, a crucial step for understanding genetics.

settings_applicationsApplications of Genetics

The knowledge gained from genetics has profound applications across various fields, significantly impacting human life and society.

a) Plant and Animal Improvement

Genetics has revolutionized agriculture and animal husbandry. Selective breeding techniques, guided by genetic principles, have led to the development of high-yielding varieties (HYV) of crops (a cornerstone of the Green Revolution) and improved livestock with enhanced milk, meat, and egg production.

b) Medicine

Genetics plays a vital role in understanding, diagnosing, and treating human diseases. Many conditions, such as hemophilia, cystic fibrosis, sickle cell anemia, certain types of diabetes, and some forms of cancer, have a genetic basis. Molecular medicine utilizes genetic information for developing targeted therapies and personalized treatments.

c) Genetic Counseling

Knowledge of inheritance patterns allows genetic counselors to assess the risk of individuals or families inheriting or passing on genetic disorders. This information helps families make informed decisions about family planning and health management.

d) Legal Applications

Genetic techniques, particularly DNA fingerprinting (also known as DNA profiling), have become indispensable tools in forensic science for criminal identification. They are also used to resolve cases of disputed parentage and in identifying victims of disasters.

e) Genetic Engineering

This field involves the direct manipulation of an organism's genes using biotechnology. Genetic engineering has led to the production of life-saving medicines like insulin, development of edible vaccines, creation of transgenic crops with desirable traits (e.g., pest resistance, enhanced nutritional value) for food security, and gene therapy approaches for treating genetic diseases.
