scienceIntroduction to Sex Determination

Sex determination is a fundamental biological system that dictates the development of sexual characteristics within an organism. Historically, sex was identified based on primary and secondary sexual characters. However, the scientific exploration of sex determination began in earnest after the discovery of sex chromosomes by McClung in 1902.

Primary sexual differentiation refers to structures directly related to the gonads (ovaries and testes). Secondary sexual differentiation encompasses the overall appearance and characteristics of an organism, such as breast development in females or beard growth in males.

Sexual differentiation is the intricate process by which differences between males and females arise from an initially undifferentiated zygote. In many species, sex determination is genetically rooted, with males and females possessing distinct chromosomes, alleles, or even specific genes that define their sexual morphology.

Across the diverse tree of life, various mechanisms of sex determination exist. Many higher organisms exhibit distinct sex phenotypes—male or female—where females produce female gametes (eggs) and males produce male gametes (sperm). Species with separate sexes in different individuals are termed dioecious.

While environmental cues like temperature, pH, or social conditions can determine the genetic sex of some organisms (e.g., turtles, snakes), a vast number of organisms rely on genetic systems. The primary modes of sex determination include:

  • Environmental Sex Determination (ESD)
  • Chromosomal Sex Determination
    • Homomorphic chromosomes (e.g., XX/XY, ZW/ZZ where chromosomes look similar)
    • Heteromorphic chromosomes (e.g., XX/XY, ZW/ZZ where X/Y or Z/W look different)
  • Genic Sex Determination (Involving single or multiple genes)

thermostatEnvironmental Sex Determination (ESD)

In some species, environmental factors play a crucial role in determining the sex of offspring. This phenomenon is known as Environmental Sex Determination (ESD).

Examples of ESD:

  • Turtles: For many turtle species, the incubation temperature of eggs is critical. Eggs incubated above a certain threshold (e.g., $32^{\circ}C$) develop into females, while those incubated below another threshold (e.g., $28^{\circ}C$) become males. Intermediate temperatures can produce a mix of sexes.
  • Bonellia viridis (Marine Worm): Larvae that settle freely on the seabed develop into females. However, if a larva settles on the proboscis (a trunk-like appendage) of an existing female, it develops into a parasitic male.
  • Coral Reef Fish: Some species of coral reef fish can change sex during their lifetime. This change can be triggered by social cues, such as the disappearance of a dominant male or female in the group.
  • Nematodes: Certain nematodes exhibit sex determination based on nutrient availability. They may develop into males if nutrients are scarce, and into females in nutrient-rich conditions, thereby enhancing the population's reproductive potential.

Key Takeaway: ESD demonstrates the fascinating interplay between an organism's development and its surrounding environment, highlighting nature's adaptability.

geneticsGenotypic/Chromosomal Sex Determination

Sexual reproduction typically involves two parents, a male and a female, each contributing a sex cell (gamete) containing half the normal amount of DNA. The male gamete is usually a sperm, and the female gamete is an egg. Fertilization occurs when these gametes fuse to form a zygote, which then develops into an embryo.

Within these gametes is one copy of each chromosome from the parent, including a sex chromosome that specifies the genetic sex of the individual. This system, known as genotypic or chromosomal sex determination, is prevalent in many species, including humans.

Heterogametic Males

In these systems, males produce two different types of gametes regarding sex chromosomes, making them "heterogametic."

1. XX-XY System

This is the system found in humans, other mammals, and certain insects like Drosophila (fruit flies).

  • Females have two X chromosomes (XX) and are homogametic, producing eggs that all carry a single X chromosome.
  • Males have one X chromosome and one Y chromosome (XY) and are heterogametic. They produce two types of sperm: half carrying an X chromosome and half carrying a Y chromosome.

The sex of the offspring is determined by the type of sperm that fertilizes the egg:

  • If an X-bearing sperm fertilizes an egg (X), the resulting zygote (XX) will be female.
  • If a Y-bearing sperm fertilizes an egg (X), the resulting zygote (XY) will be male.

Diagram: XX-XY Sex Determination (Punnett Square)

Mother (XX) produces X eggs. Father (XY) produces X and Y sperm.

Offspring: XX (Female), XX (Female), XY (Male), XY (Male) - 50% chance of each.

The Y chromosome carries the SRY gene (Sex-determining Region Y), which acts as a master switch. It initiates the release of testosterone and the development of male sex organs. In the absence of a functional SRY gene, the gonads typically develop into ovaries.

2. XX-XO System

Found in certain plants and insects like grasshoppers and bugs.

  • Females have two X chromosomes (XX) and are homogametic.
  • Males have only one X chromosome (XO). The "O" signifies the absence of a second sex chromosome. Males are heterogametic, producing sperm, half with an X chromosome and half with no sex chromosome.

Sex determination:

  • Egg (X) + Sperm (X) = XX (Female)
  • Egg (X) + Sperm (O) = XO (Male)

Heterogametic Females

In these systems, females produce two different types of gametes regarding sex chromosomes.

1. ZO-ZZ System

Found in certain moths and butterflies.

  • Females have one Z chromosome and no W chromosome (ZO). They are heterogametic, producing two types of eggs: half with a Z chromosome and half with no Z chromosome.
  • Males have two Z chromosomes (ZZ) and are homogametic, producing sperm that all carry a Z chromosome.

The sex of the offspring depends on the egg:

  • Egg (Z) + Sperm (Z) = ZZ (Male)
  • Egg (O) + Sperm (Z) = ZO (Female)

2. ZW-ZZ System

This system is found in birds, some fishes, reptiles, and certain insects.

  • Females have one Z chromosome and one W chromosome (ZW) and are heterogametic. They produce two types of eggs: 50% with a Z chromosome and 50% with a W chromosome.
  • Males have two Z chromosomes (ZZ) and are homogametic, producing sperm that all carry a Z chromosome.

The sex of the offspring is determined by the egg, making the mother's sex chromosome decisive:

  • Egg (Z) + Sperm (Z) = ZZ (Male)
  • Egg (W) + Sperm (Z) = ZW (Female)

Similar to the SRY gene on the mammalian Y chromosome, the bird W chromosome carries master switch genes like FET1 and ASW, which are essential for female development.

Haplo-Diploid System (Haplodiploidy)

This unique system is found in insects like honey bees, ants, and wasps.

  • Females (queens and workers) develop from fertilized eggs and are diploid (2n), meaning they have two sets of chromosomes (e.g., 32 in honey bees).
  • Males (drones) develop from unfertilized eggs (parthenogenesis) and are haploid (n), having only one set of chromosomes (e.g., 16 in honey bees).

The queen bee produces haploid eggs through meiosis. If an egg is fertilized by sperm (which are produced by haploid males via mitosis, not meiosis), it develops into a diploid female. If an egg remains unfertilized, it develops into a haploid male.

Diagram: Haplo-Diploid Sex Determination in Honey Bees

(Illustrating fertilized eggs becoming diploid females (queen/worker) and unfertilized eggs becoming haploid males (drones), similar to diagrams on page 3 & 4 of "sex determination.pdf")

An interesting consequence is that a male bee has a grandfather and can have grandsons, but he has no father and cannot have sons in the traditional sense.

balanceGenic Balance System

The Genic Balance Theory, notably studied in Drosophila melanogaster (fruit fly) by Calvin Bridges, proposes that sex is determined by the balance between genes for maleness (primarily on autosomes) and genes for femaleness (primarily on the X chromosome).

The sex of an individual depends on the ratio of X chromosomes to sets of autosomes (X/A ratio or sex index):

  • X/A ratio = 1.0 (e.g., XX in a diploid, 2X:2A): Develops into a normal female.
  • X/A ratio = 0.5 (e.g., XY in a diploid, 1X:2A): Develops into a normal male. (Note: In Drosophila, the Y chromosome is important for male fertility but not primary sex determination).
  • X/A ratio > 1.0 (e.g., 3X:2A = 1.5): Develops into a "superfemale" (metafemale), often sterile and with developmental issues.
  • X/A ratio < 0.5 (e.g., 1X:3A = 0.33): Develops into a "supermale" (metamale), often sterile.
  • X/A ratio between 0.5 and 1.0 (e.g., 2X:3A = 0.67): Develops into an intersex individual, showing characteristics of both sexes.

merge_typeGynandromorphy

A gynandromorph is an organism that exhibits both male and female characteristics. The term derives from Greek: gyne (female), andro (male), and morph (form). This phenomenon often results from chromosomal anomalies during early development, such as the loss of a sex chromosome in a cell lineage.

Types of Gynandromorphs:

  • Bilateral Gynandromorphs: One half of the body displays female characteristics, while the other half displays male characteristics. This is often strikingly visible in insects like butterflies or birds with sexually dimorphic coloration.
  • Anterior-Posterior Gynandromorphs: The anterior (front) region of the body has characteristics of one sex, and the posterior (rear) region has characteristics of the other sex.
  • Sex Piebalds (Mosaic Gynandromorphs): The body consists predominantly of tissue of one sex, with patches of tissue characteristic of the other sex scattered irregularly.

Gynandromorphy provides valuable insights into the genetic and developmental processes underlying sexual differentiation.

ecoSex Determination in Plants

Sex determination in plants is remarkably diverse. While many plants are hermaphroditic (bisexual), having both male (stamens) and female (carpels/pistils) reproductive organs in the same flower, others have evolved mechanisms for separate sexes.

  • Monoecious plants: Have separate male and female flowers on the same individual plant (e.g., maize, castor, coconut).
  • Dioecious plants: Have male and female flowers on different individual plants (e.g., papaya, spinach, hops, asparagus). This necessitates cross-pollination.

Environmental Sex Determination in Plants

Similar to some animals, environmental factors can influence sex expression in certain plants:

  • Equisetum (Horsetail): Spores may develop into female gametophytes under optimal conditions and male gametophytes under adverse conditions.
  • Cucumber, Melons, Cannabis: Sex expression can be affected by temperature, day length, and plant hormones like ethylene (promotes female flowers) and gibberellins (can promote male flowers in some cases, or female in others like cannabis).

Chromosomal Sex Determination in Plants

Some dioecious plants utilize sex chromosome systems, analogous to those in animals.

1. Heteromorphic Sex Chromosomes (X and Y morphologically distinct)
  • XX/XY System (Male Heterogamety):
    • Females are XX, Males are XY.
    • Found in species like Melandrium album (white campion), Silene latifolia, Cannabis sativa (hemp), Rumex hastatulus.
    • In Melandrium, the Y chromosome is a strong male determinant. Different regions of the Y chromosome control various aspects of male development (e.g., suppression of femaleness, promotion of maleness, male fertility). The X chromosome carries genes for femaleness.

      Diagram: X and Y chromosomes in Melandrium album

      (Showing different segments I, II, III, IV, V controlling sex determination, similar to page 6 of "sex determination.pdf" and page 7 of "S.D.(edited).pdf")

  • Complex XY Systems (e.g., $X_1X_1X_2X_2$ Female / $X_1X_2Y_1Y_2$ Male):
    • Found in some strains of Humulus lupulus (hops).
    • Females produce $X_1X_2$ eggs. Males produce $X_1X_2$ and $Y_1Y_2$ pollen.
2. Homomorphic Sex Chromosomes (X and Y morphologically similar but functionally different)
  • XX/XY System (Male Heterogamety):
    • Found in Spinacia oleracea (spinach) and Asparagus officinalis.
    • Although X and Y chromosomes are morphologically similar, the Y chromosome often carries genes suppressing carpel development and promoting stamen development. YY males have been produced in Asparagus.
  • XY/XX System (Female Heterogamety):
    • Females are XY, Males are XX.
    • Reported in some species like Fragaria elatior (wild strawberry). Females are heterogametic.

The X/Autosome ratio can also play a role in some plant systems, though often the Y chromosome has a more dominant effect than in Drosophila.

Genic Sex Determination in Plants

In many plants, sex is controlled by one or a few major genes, rather than entire sex chromosomes.

  • Single Locus Systems:
    • Spitting Cucumber (Ecballium elaterium): A single gene 'a' with three alleles ($a^D$ for monoecious, $a^+$ for male, $a^d$ for female) controls sex, with a dominance hierarchy ($a^D > a^+ > a^d$).
    • Papaya (Carica papaya): A single gene with three alleles (M1 for male, M2 for hermaphrodite, m for female) determines sex. Genotypes $M_1M_1$, $M_2M_2$, and $M_1M_2$ are lethal.
      • mm: Female
      • $M_1m$: Male
      • $M_2m$: Hermaphrodite
  • Multiple Loci Systems / Gene Interactions:
    • Maize (Zea mays): A monoecious plant.
      • Recessive gene ba (barren stalk) in homozygous state (baba) makes cobs (female inflorescences) non-functional, effectively creating a male plant.
      • Recessive gene ts (tassel seed) in homozygous state (tsts) converts tassels (male inflorescences) into female flowers, effectively creating a female plant.
      • A baba tsts plant would be female (female tassel, barren cob).
    • Cucumber (Cucumis sativus) and Melon (Cucumis melo): Complex interactions of genes (e.g., M/m, F/f, A/a, G/g) determine different sex phenotypes like gynoecious (all female flowers), monoecious, andromonoecious (male and hermaphrodite flowers), hermaphrodite.

Metabolic Differentiation: Proposed by Riddle, suggesting that metabolic states, like carbohydrate or nitrogen deficiency, could influence sex expression in plants like tomato or cucumber, often by affecting hormone levels.

filter_vintageUV Sex Determination System

The UV sex determination system is found in some lower plants like bryophytes (mosses, liverworts, hornworts) and certain algae. This system is distinct because it operates in the haploid phase of the life cycle.

  • Individuals are haploid and possess only one sex chromosome.
  • Females carry a single U chromosome.
  • Males carry a single V chromosome.

Unlike the XY and ZW systems where one chromosome is often largely inert or primarily carries the sex-determining gene (Y or W), in the UV system, both the U and V chromosomes are considered sex-determining.

In bryophytes, a diploid sporophyte produces haploid spores through meiosis. These spores, carrying either a U or a V chromosome, grow into haploid gametophytes (the dominant phase of the life cycle). The U-bearing gametophyte is female and produces eggs, while the V-bearing gametophyte is male and produces sperm. Fertilization of an egg by a sperm (both haploid) results in a diploid zygote, which develops into the sporophyte, completing the cycle.

starImportance of Sex Determination

Understanding sex determination systems is crucial for several reasons:

  • Evolution and Adaptation: Sexual reproduction, facilitated by distinct sexes, allows for genetic recombination, leading to increased variation within populations. This variation is the raw material for evolution and helps species adapt to changing environments.
  • Genetic Health: Sexual processes can combine beneficial mutations and help eliminate deleterious mutations from a population more efficiently than asexual reproduction.
  • Crop Improvement: In agriculture, knowing the sex determination mechanism of crop plants is vital.
    • For dioecious fruit crops (e.g., papaya, date palm, kiwi), ensuring a proper ratio of male to female plants is necessary for pollination and fruit set.
    • Understanding sex determination can aid in breeding programs, for example, to develop gynoecious lines in cucumbers (all-female flowers, leading to higher yields) or to manage pollination in hybrid seed production.
    • In many crops like melon, squash, strawberries, and grapes, only female or hermaphrodite flowers produce fruit.
  • Species Conservation: For endangered species, understanding their sex determination system can be critical for captive breeding programs and conservation efforts.
  • Pest Control: Manipulating sex determination or sex ratios can be a strategy in controlling certain insect pests.
  • Preventing Inbreeding Depression: In flowering plants, various strategies for sex determination (like dioecy) have evolved to promote outcrossing and prevent self-fertilization, which can lead to inbreeding depression (loss of fitness).
  • Basic Biological Research: Studying the diverse mechanisms of sex determination provides fundamental insights into gene regulation, developmental biology, and evolution. Sex determination systems can evolve relatively rapidly, making them interesting models for evolutionary studies.

transgenderSex-Limited Traits

Sex-limited traits are characteristics that are expressed in only one sex, even though the genes responsible for them may be present in both sexes. These genes are typically located on autosomes (non-sex chromosomes), and their expression is controlled by sex hormones or other physiological differences between males and females.

Key Characteristics:

  • Genes are autosomal.
  • Expression occurs in only one sex (zero penetrance in the other sex).
  • The same genotype produces different phenotypes in males versus females due to hormonal environments.

Examples:

  • Lactation (Milk Production): Genes for milk production are present in both male and female mammals, but milk is typically only produced by females after parturition due to hormonal influences (e.g., prolactin, estrogen).
  • Beard Growth in Humans: The genes for beard growth are present in both sexes, but significant beard development is typically limited to males due to higher levels of androgens.
  • Plumage Differences in Birds: Elaborate and colorful plumage in males of many bird species (e.g., peacocks, birds of paradise) is a sex-limited trait, while females often have more cryptic coloration.
  • Horn Development in Some Sheep Breeds: In certain sheep breeds, horns may develop only in males, or be significantly larger in males, even if both sexes carry the genes for horn growth.
  • Prostate Gland in Males / Ovarian Development in Females: These are anatomical structures limited to one sex. Conditions like prostate cancer are male-limited, and ovarian cancer is female-limited, though genes predisposing to these can be carried by either sex.
  • Barred Coloring in Chickens: This pattern is typically visible only in roosters in some breeds.
  • Tone of Voice: The deeper voice in adult human males is a sex-limited trait influenced by testosterone's effect on the larynx.
  • Hairy Ears (Hypertrichosis Pinnae Auris): While sometimes cited as Y-linked, some sources consider pronounced ear hair development as a sex-limited trait influenced by androgens, becoming more apparent in males with age. (Note: True Y-linkage means the gene is on the Y chromosome.)

wcSex-Influenced Traits

Sex-influenced traits (also called sex-conditioned traits) are traits whose expression is conditioned or influenced by the sex of the individual. The genes for these traits are typically autosomal, but the trait appears differently or with different frequencies in males and females, usually due to hormonal differences.

Unlike sex-limited traits (where the trait is expressed in only one sex), sex-influenced traits can appear in both sexes but are more common or more pronounced in one sex than the other. The allele for the trait might behave as dominant in one sex and recessive in the other.

Key Characteristics:

  • Genes are autosomal.
  • Expressed in both sexes, but to different degrees or frequencies.
  • An allele can be dominant in one sex and recessive in the other.

Examples:

  • Pattern Baldness (Androgenic Alopecia) in Humans: This is a classic example. The allele for baldness (B) behaves as dominant in males but recessive in females.
    • Males: Genotypes BB and Bb will lead to baldness. Only bb males will not be bald.
    • Females: Only genotype BB will lead to significant hair thinning/baldness. Bb females usually have normal hair or minimal thinning, and bb females have normal hair. Higher levels of androgens in males contribute to this pattern.
  • Horn Presence in Some Sheep Breeds: In some breeds, an allele for horns might be dominant in males (males with one copy are horned) but recessive in females (females need two copies to be horned).
  • Length of Index Finger vs. Ring Finger: Some studies suggest this ratio can be a sex-influenced trait, with males more often having a shorter index finger relative to the ring finger, possibly influenced by prenatal androgen exposure.
  • Certain Autoimmune Diseases: Some autoimmune diseases like lupus or rheumatoid arthritis are more prevalent in females, suggesting sex hormones may influence their development or expression.
  • Soft Facial Hair in Females vs. Coarse Facial Hair in Males: While significant beard growth is sex-limited to males, the general texture and amount of facial hair can be considered sex-influenced.

linkSex-Linked Traits

Sex-linked traits are determined by genes located on the sex chromosomes (X or Y in humans/mammals; Z or W in birds/some insects). The inheritance patterns of these traits differ from autosomal traits because they are linked to the inheritance of sex chromosomes.

1. X-Linked Traits

Genes located on the X chromosome. Since males (XY) have only one X chromosome, they are hemizygous for X-linked genes. This means that a single recessive allele on the X chromosome will be expressed in males. Females (XX) have two X chromosomes, so a recessive allele on one X can be masked by a dominant allele on the other X (making them carriers).

X-Linked Recessive Traits:
  • More frequently expressed in males than females.
  • Affected males inherit the recessive allele from their carrier or affected mother. They cannot pass the trait to their sons (because sons get the Y chromosome from their father), but all their daughters will be carriers if the mother is homozygous normal.
  • Examples:
    • Red-Green Color Blindness: Difficulty distinguishing between red and green hues.
    • Hemophilia A and B: Blood clotting disorders.
    • Duchenne Muscular Dystrophy (DMD): Progressive muscle degeneration.
X-Linked Dominant Traits:
  • Less common than X-linked recessive traits.
  • Affected males pass the trait to all their daughters but none of their sons.
  • Affected heterozygous females pass the trait to 50% of their offspring (both sons and daughters).
  • Affected homozygous females pass the trait to all their offspring.
  • Examples:
    • Hypophosphatemic Rickets (Vitamin D-resistant rickets)
    • Rett Syndrome (primarily in females, often lethal in males)

2. Y-Linked Traits (Holandric Traits)

Genes located on the Y chromosome. These traits are only found in males and are passed directly from father to all his sons. The Y chromosome is relatively small and contains fewer genes compared to the X chromosome, many of which are related to male fertility (like the SRY gene).

  • Example:
    • Hairy Ears (Hypertrichosis Pinnae Auris): Some forms are thought to be Y-linked, though this is debated and may also be influenced by autosomal genes with sex-limited expression.
    • SRY gene itself is a Y-linked gene critical for male development.

Understanding sex-linked inheritance is crucial for genetic counseling and predicting the probability of offspring inheriting certain genetic conditions.
