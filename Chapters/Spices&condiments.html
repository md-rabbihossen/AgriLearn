Introduction to Spices & Condiments

Welcome to the comprehensive guide on Spices and Condiments. This documentation explores the fascinating world of these essential culinary ingredients, their classifications, uses, and cultivation.

What are Spices?

Spices are plant products primarily used to flavor food due to their distinct aromas and processing properties. They can make food more pungent, stimulating, or pleasantly sharp. Spices often contain essential oils that provide flavor and taste, and many can increase the shelf life of food. They are used whole, ground, as a paste, or in liquid form.

According to the International Organization for Standardization (ISO), spices and condiments refer to natural plant or vegetable products (or mixtures) used in whole or ground form for imparting flavor, aroma, piquancy, or taste, and for seasoning foods.

There is no clear cut division between 'spices' and 'condiments' and as such they have been clubbed together.

General Grouping

Spices are generally divided into three groups:

  • Spice
  • Condiment
  • Culinary herb

A spice is typically used during cooking or baking to add flavor, aroma, color, and texture. Examples include onion, garlic, turmeric, ginger, chili, cardamom, and cinnamon. Spices can also be used in medicine, religious rituals, cosmetics, or perfume production.

Functions and Properties of Spices:

  • Act as antioxidants.
  • Act as antimicrobial compounds.
  • Added at the initial stage of food preparation.
  • Can be simple or complex, and used in condiment preparation.

Did You Know?

Many spices not only enhance flavor but also possess significant health benefits due to their antioxidant and antimicrobial properties!

What are Condiments?

Condiments are prepared food compounds containing one or more spices or spice extracts. They are typically added to food after cooking or at the table to enhance flavor or quality. Examples include barbecue sauce, chili sauce, chutney, ketchup, mustard, soy sauce, and vinegar.

Functions and Properties of Condiments:

  • Enhance color and flavor.
  • The condiment trade is a significant market in specialty foods.
  • Food ingredients such as salt, mustard, or pickle are used to add only taste to the food.
  • Added immediately before serving to enhance the taste of the food.
  • Categorized based on their botany.

Key Differences: Spices vs. Condiments

Feature Spices Condiments
Usage Time A spice is basically used during the cooking or baking preparation and process to add flavor, aroma, color, and texture to a certain dish/food. A condiment is used after cooking or at the table when serving to improve its flavor, color, aroma, and texture.
Nature A spice is technically a single culinary element. Condiments are usually considered supplementary components of food.
Examples Cumin seeds, Cinnamon, Nutmeg, Coriander seeds, Ginger, Garlic, Cloves, onion etc. Barbecue sauce, Ketchup, Red chili paste, Ranch dressing, Butter, Mayonnaise etc.

Culinary Herbs

When leaves or twigs (in their green stage) are used to stimulate the taste of food, they are called culinary herbs. Examples include mint, coriander, and curry leaf.

Quick Tip!

Culinary herbs are often best added towards the end of cooking or used fresh to preserve their delicate flavors.

Classification of Spices: Overview

Spices can be classified in different ways depending on plant parts used, the economic importance, climatic requirements, origin and flavour, requirement of season and botanical description.

Classification: Based on Parts Used

Spices can be classified depending on the parts of the plant that are to be used. Different plant parts like leaf, root, bulb, fruit, seed, etc. are used as spice.

  • a) Seed: Cumin, black cumin, fenugreek, coriander, fennel, ajowan, poppy, aniseed and mustard.
  • b) Bulb: Onion, garlic, leek and shallot.
  • c) Bark: Cinnamon and cassia.
  • d) Fruit: Chilli, cardamom and kokam.
  • e) Leaf: Mint, curry leaf, bay leaf, chive, rosemary and savory.
  • f) Rhizome: Turmeric, ginger, and galangal.
  • g) Pod: Vanilla and tamarind.
  • h) Kernel: Nutmeg.
  • i) Floral part: Saffron, savory, caper and marjoram.
  • j) Bud: Clove and caper.
  • k) Latex: Asafetida.
  • l) Aril: Mace and anardana.

Classification: Based on Economic Importance

On the basis of economic importance of the spices can be grouped into two viz, major and minor spices.

I. Major spices

The spices which contribute major share in the spice trade industry of the world are called major spices. The spices come under this group are small cardamom, black pepper, chilli, turmeric and ginger. These spices contribute about 75-90% of the total foreign exchange earned through spices.

II. Minor spices

Excluding all these five major spices, all other are called minor spices. Minor spices are further divided into five sub groups. They are mentioned hereunder:

  • a. Seed spices: Coriander, cumin, black cumin, fennel, aniseed, celery, mustard, poppy and caraway
  • b. Bulbous spices: Garlic, onion, leek and shallot
  • c. Aromatic spices: Clove, cinnamon, aniseed and nutmeg
  • d. Leafy spices: Curry leaf, mint, rosemary, bayleaf, and parsley.
  • e. Acidulant tree spices: Tamarind, kokam and anardana.

Classification: Based on Climatic Requirement

Depending on suitable climatic conditions like temperature, sunlight, humidity and air of a particular climatic zone, spices are grouped into three categories.

Tropical spices

Spices of this category need high temperature, and abundant humidity. They are easily damaged by low temperature. Tropical spices are ginger, turmeric, black pepper, cinnamon, kokam, galangal, small cardamom and clove.

Subtropical spices

Sub-tropical climate is found where three distinct seasons like winter, summer and monsoon are found. Low temperature occurs in winter and high temperature during summer. Most of the spices require relatively low temperature during their vegetative or early growth stage and high temperature in reproductive stage. The examples of sub-tropical spices grown in winter are cumin, fennel, coriander, fenugreek, onion and garlic. Subtropical spices grown during summer are turmeric and ginger.

Temperate spices

Spices of this type can withstand low temperature and frosty weather but are damaged easily in hot weather. Examples of temperate spices are thymes, saffron, savoy, caraway seed and asfoetida.

Classification: Based on Origin and Flavor

Depending on the origin and flavour content of the spices, they can be classified as follows:

  • Aromatic spices: Cardamom, aniseed, celery, cumin, coriander, fenugreek and cinnamon.
  • Pungent spices: Ginger, chilli, black pepper and mustard.
  • Phenolic spices: Clove.
  • Coloured spices: Turmeric, saffron and paprika.

Classification: Based on Growing Season

According to the requirement of season of growth, spices are grouped into following three classes:

  • Annual spices: Spices which complete their life cycle in one growing season are called annuals. Example of this type of spices is coriander, cumin, fennel, fenugreek, ajowan and black cumin.
  • Biennial spices: It needs two growing seasons to complete the life cycle. Examples of biennial spices are onion and parsley.
  • Perennial spices: Perennial spices are those which live for more than two years. Black pepper, saffron, clove, nutmeg and cinnamon are example of perennial spices.

Importance of Spices

The role of spices in social, cultural and economic evolution of our country is well known.

  • The spices are being used day to day in the family at all occasions of sorrow, happiness and festivals in one or the other forms. e.g. clove, cardamom etc.
  • They contribute to the national economy by way of export earning.
  • They are indispensable part of our culinary preparations especially used for flavoring and coloring of food.
  • Some spices are used as preservatives in some food items like chutneys and pickles etc.
  • Most of the spices have potential medicinal values. The spice and spice products are used as medicines and in many pharmaceutical preparations.
  • Spices have been used in cosmetic perfumery industries. Spice oils are used in the manufacture of soaps, tooth paste, talcum powder, aftershave lotions, vanishing creams, mouth and room fresheners etc.
  • Spices are used as the raw material for several cottage and heavy industries.
  • Spice crops also protect the soil surface from erosion as a result to water and wind force.

Problems in Spice Cultivation

  • There is no good cultivar available to the farmer. e.g. cumin
  • Production cost is high.
  • Lack of availability of seed such as onion, garlic, cumin etc.
  • Lack of improved cultural method
  • Lack of awareness and literacy.
  • Propagation problem.
  • High input cost.
  • Lack of extension motivation.
  • Food security: Although profit may be high, in times of food security, a farmer and his family can not depend on the spices to carry him, through to the next harvest. A farmer's first concern is food security which in Bangladesh means rice security.
  • Low postharvest prices: Most of spices double or triple in price between postharvest and off season but only a few farmers are able to realize the benefits.

Scope for Spice Cultivation

  • Although spice cover less than 1.88% of the total annual cropped area of the country, here importance to individual farmer is far greater then what the national statistics indicate. In some area, the economy of the population is dependent on the cultivation of spices eg. commercial spices production tend to be concentrated in particular areas, for example Faridpur for onion, garlic and coriander, Chittagong hill tract for zinger and turmeric, chilli production in Bogra and Comilla and Rangpur/ Dinajpur for all the major spices.
  • Favorable agro climate conditions in Bangladesh make expansion of spice production, both through yield and area feasible.
  • Spice crops can better fit in the cropping pattern of an area or zone to maintain the soil fertility.
  • The cost of production of these spice crops is relatively less than cereals and other crops.
  • Most of the spice crops are shade loving and can be grown in the space between and within rows of a young or well established orchards and provide security to the farmers as one crop fails this may rescue the farmers.
  • Spices will remain an essential part of the diet in Bangladesh retail prices determining the total quality demanded.
  • These can be grown where ever possible as the growers get more returns per hectare from spice crops per unit area, time and inputs invested than cereals and other commodity crops.
  • Spices help in providing employment opportunities and above all a huge amount of foreign exchange is earned through export of these spices and spice products. They are very useful remunerative crops and they should be grown intensively in the country.

Present Situations of Spices (Focus on Bangladesh)

Spices and condiments have great signification for their diversified uses in our daily life. In our country, major spices like onion, chilli, zinger, turmeric, garlic, coriander seed etc although 17 different kinds of spices are produced in the area of about 3.48 lac hectare which is only about 1.88% total cultivable land. The total requirement of the country is about 16 lac mt per annum where as, the production is about 28% of our total requirement.

The existing gap of the production and the requirement is now mitigated every year by spending valuable foreign currency. Some spices such as cinnamon (1500 mt), cumin (5000 mt), cardamom (500 mt), clove (500 mt) etc are totally depend on import. Yearly 150 crore spent for import of these spices and except those additional 30 crore are spent for import of poppy seed, wild mace, saffron, black pepper etc. There are about 107 spices in use all over the world and 20 countries actively engage in the production and export of one or more of them. We should consume per head $32~g/day$ but our availability only $8g/day$.

Most the spice crops are grown well in our climate but their low yield due to use of local variety, use of traditional cultivation and storage techniques etc are the limiting factors. Keeping the above fact, we can say that if we develop high yielding modern variety as well as modern storage techniques of different spice crops then we can solve our existing gap of production and requirements.

Cardamom (Queen of Spices)

Cardamoms of commerce one the dried capsules of a group of species or plants belonging to the family Zingiberaceae, which contain seeds, possessing a pleasant characteristic aroma. Cardamoms are broadly grouped into two categories- (a) Small cardamom: Popularly known as chhota Elaichi or the true cardamom (Elettaria cardamomum) and (b) Large cardamom: Bara Elaichi (Aframomum and Amomum species).

Key Varieties of Elettaria cardamomum:

  • Malabar: Medium-sized plants (2-3m), prostrate panicles, small globose fruits.
  • Mysore: Robust plants (3-4m), erect panicles, fusiform fruits.
  • Vazhuka: Natural hybrid of Malabar and Mysore types.

Uses:

  • Flavoring foods, confectionery, beverages, and liquors.
  • Medicinal purposes (Allopathic and Ayurvedic).
  • Preparation of 'Gahwa' (Arab Coffee) in Middle Eastern countries.

Turmeric (Golden Spice)

Turmeric (Curcuma longa), from the Zingiberaceae family, is a vital tropical spice. India is its largest producer. Key species include C. longa (widely cultivated), C. aromatica (Cochin turmeric), C. angustifolia (East Indian arrowroot), and C. amada (mango ginger).

Uses:

  • Important yellow dye (for cotton, silk, wool).
  • Cosmetics (turmeric powder and water).
  • Preservative and coloring in pickles, cheese.
  • Ceremonial rice coloring.
  • Medicine (Ayurvedic and Unani systems): stomachic, tonic, blood purifier.
  • Treating gall stones.
  • Coloring in pharmacy, confectionery, food industries.
  • Toilet item for skin cleaning, prevents skin infection.
  • Claimed to suppress unwanted hair growth.
  • Converted into kumkum.

The characteristic yellow color of turmeric comes from curcumin, a phenolic pigment. The aroma is due to turmerone.

Onion (Queen of the Kitchen)

Onion (Allium cepa) from the Alliaceae family, has been used as food and medicine since ancient times. It's grown in 170 countries and is valued for its peculiar taste, unique flavor, and health benefits. It ranks first in production among spices in Bangladesh.

Cinnamon (Aromatic Bark)

Cinnamon (Cinnamomum zeylanicum) from the Lauraceae family, consists of dried inner bark pieces. True cinnamon is distinct from Cassia, which is a common substitute. Sri Lankan cinnamon is considered among the best.
