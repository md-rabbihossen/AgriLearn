Concept of Gene

Introduction to the Concept of Gene

The journey into understanding heredity has been long and fascinating. At its core lies the gene, the fundamental unit of inheritance. This section explores the historical context and foundational definitions of a gene.

Historical Perspectives & Definitions

The term gene was formally introduced by Danish botanist Wilhelm Johanssen in 1909. He defined a gene as an elementary unit of inheritance that could be assigned to a particular trait.

However, the concept predates Johanssen. Gregor Mendel, in his groundbreaking work with pea plants (published in 1866, though recognized later), used the term "factor" to describe these distinct, particulate units of inheritance responsible for the expression of traits.

Later, Thomas Hunt Morgan's work with fruit flies in the early 20th century provided further insights. His research suggested that genes were the shortest segments of a chromosome that could be separated through crossing over, undergo mutation, and influence the expression of one or more traits.

What is a Gene? - Modern Understanding

Presently, a gene is more precisely defined as:

A unit of inheritance composed of a segment of DNA (or RNA in some viruses) located at a specific position (locus) on a chromosome. This segment carries coded information associated with a specific function (e.g., the synthesis of a protein or a functional RNA molecule) and can undergo crossing over as well as mutation.

A gene can be characterized by several key properties:

  • Unit of Replication: It is a segment of genetic material that is able to replicate, ensuring its passage to subsequent generations.
  • Unit of Recombination: It is capable of undergoing crossing over, leading to new combinations of genetic material.
  • Unit of Mutation: It can undergo changes (mutations) in its DNA sequence, potentially leading to altered traits.
  • Unit of Heredity & Function: It is fundamentally connected with somatic structure or function, ultimately leading to a phenotypic expression (an observable characteristic).

Modern Concept of Gene: Cistron, Recon, Muton

The discovery of DNA as the genetic material revolutionized the understanding of genes. The classical concept of a gene as an indivisible unit was refined. It became clear that a gene could be further divided into smaller units related to its function, its ability to recombine, and its susceptibility to mutation.

In 1955, Seymour Benzer, through his work with bacteriophages (viruses that infect bacteria), coined three crucial terms to explain the relationship between DNA structure and genetic phenomena at a finer scale:

a) Cistron: The Unit of Function

  • The cistron represents a segment of the DNA molecule that specifies a single functional unit, typically a polypeptide chain (protein) or a functional RNA molecule (like tRNA or rRNA).
  • It consists of a linear sequence of nucleotides.
  • A cistron effectively defines the boundaries of a gene in terms of its functional output. It usually begins with an initiation codon (start signal) and ends with a termination codon (stop signal).
  • Each cistron is responsible for coding one messenger RNA (mRNA) molecule, which in turn directs the synthesis of one polypeptide chain.
  • A cistron can be thought of as encompassing hundreds of mutons and recons.

b) Recon: The Unit of Recombination

  • The recon is the smallest segment of DNA that is capable of undergoing genetic recombination (e.g., through crossing over).
  • Recombination studies, particularly in microbes, indicated that a recon could be as small as one or two pairs of nucleotides, possibly even a single nucleotide pair. This means that recombination can occur *within* a gene, not just between genes.

c) Muton: The Unit of Mutation

  • The muton is the shortest chromosomal unit or segment of DNA whose alteration can result in a mutation and a consequent change in phenotype.
  • Like the recon, a muton can be very small, potentially consisting of a single nucleotide pair. A change in even one nucleotide can lead to a mutation.

Key takeaway: Benzer's work demonstrated that the gene is not an indivisible "bead on a string" but a divisible segment of DNA with distinct regions for function, recombination, and mutation.

Types of Genes

Genes can be classified in various ways based on their activity, function, structure, and regulation. Understanding these classifications helps in appreciating the diverse roles genes play within an organism.

1. House Keeping Genes (Constitutive Genes)

These genes are constantly expressing themselves in a cell because their products are essential for normal cellular activities and survival. They are always "on."

  • Examples: Genes coding for enzymes involved in glycolysis (basic energy production), ATPase (energy currency), ribosomal proteins, or cytoskeletal proteins.

2. Non-constitutive Genes (Luxury Genes or Regulated Genes)

These genes are not always expressing themselves. Their expression is regulated, meaning they can be switched "on" or "off" according to the specific needs of the cell or organism at a particular time or in response to environmental cues.

  • Examples: Gene for nitrate reductase in plants (only active when nitrate is available), lactose metabolism (lac operon) genes in Escherichia coli (only active when lactose is present).

Non-constitutive genes are further divided into:

a) Inducible Genes

These genes are normally "off" but are switched "on" (induced) in response to the presence of a specific chemical substance called an inducer. The inducer is often a substrate for the pathway controlled by the gene product.

  • Example: The lac operon genes in E. coli are induced by lactose (or its isomer allolactose). Nitrate acts as an inducer for the nitrate reductase gene.

b) Repressible Genes

These genes are normally "on" but are switched "off" (repressed) when a specific chemical, often an end product of a metabolic pathway, accumulates. This inhibition by an end product is known as feedback repression.

  • Example: Genes involved in the synthesis of an amino acid (e.g., tryptophan operon in E. coli) are repressed when sufficient tryptophan is available.

3. Multigenes (Multiple Gene Family)

This refers to a group of similar or nearly similar genes that often arise from gene duplication events. They typically code for products with related functions and may be expressed at different times or in different tissues to meet specific requirements.

  • Example: The globin gene family, which includes genes for different forms of hemoglobin (e.g., embryonic, fetal, adult hemoglobin) that are expressed at different developmental stages.

4. Repeated Genes

These genes occur in multiple copies (sometimes hundreds or thousands) within the genome because their products are required in large quantities by the cell.

  • Examples: Genes for ribosomal RNA (rRNA), transfer RNA (tRNA), histone proteins (which package DNA), and actin (a cytoskeletal protein).

5. Single Copy Genes

These genes are present in only one copy (or occasionally 2-3 copies) per haploid genome. The majority of protein-coding genes in eukaryotes fall into this category.

  • They form about 60-70% of the functional genes.
  • New genes can arise from single copy genes through processes like duplication, mutation, and exon reshuffling.

6. Pseudogenes (ψ)

Pseudogenes are DNA sequences that show high homology (similarity) to functional genes but are unable to produce functional products. They are essentially "disabled" or "fossil" genes.

  • Reasons for non-functionality include the accumulation of mutations such as intervening nonsense (stop) codons, insertions, deletions, or inactivation of promoter regions.
  • Example: Several of the small nuclear RNA (snRNA) genes have pseudogene counterparts. Many olfactory receptor genes in humans are pseudogenes compared to other mammals with a keener sense of smell.

7. Processed Genes (Retrogenes)

These are eukaryotic genes that lack introns (non-coding sequences). They are thought to have been formed by reverse transcription of a mature mRNA molecule (which has already had its introns spliced out) back into DNA, followed by reinsertion of this DNA copy into the genome.

  • This process often involves enzymes like reverse transcriptase, which can be provided by retroviruses or endogenous retrotransposons.
  • Processed genes are generally non-functional because they often lack the necessary promoter sequences for transcription, or they are inserted into regions of the genome where they are not expressed.

8. Split Genes (Genes with Introns and Exons)

Discovered in 1977 by several groups, with credit often given to Philip Sharp and Richard Roberts (who shared the Nobel Prize for this discovery).

Split genes are characterized by having their coding sequences (exons) interrupted by non-coding sequences (introns, also known as intervening sequences or IVS, or spacer DNA).

  • Exons: The essential or coding parts of the gene that are expressed and ultimately translated into protein (or form a functional RNA).
  • Introns: The non-essential or non-coding parts that are transcribed into the initial RNA (pre-mRNA) but are then removed through a process called RNA splicing before the mature RNA leaves the nucleus.
  • Split genes are a hallmark of eukaryotic genomes.
  • However, some eukaryotic genes are entirely exonic (non-split), such as histone genes and interferon genes.
  • Split genes have also been found in some prokaryotes and viruses (e.g., thymidylate synthase gene and ribonucleotide reductase gene in bacteriophage T4).
  • Alternative Splicing: A single gene can produce multiple different proteins by differentially splicing its exons. For example, a gene that produces the hormone calcitonin in the thyroid gland can form a different neuropeptide in the hypothalamus by removing an exon during splicing. Adenoviruses can produce 15-20 different proteins from a single transcriptional unit through differential splicing.

9. Transposons (Jumping Genes)

First conceptualized by Barbara McClintock in 1951 from her work with maize (corn), for which she later received the Nobel Prize. The term "transposon" was coined by Hedges and Jacob in 1974.

Transposons are segments of DNA that have the ability to move (transpose) from one location in the genome to another. This "jumping" can occur within the same chromosome or to a different chromosome.

  • McClintock observed that a segment of DNA moved into a gene coding for pigmented kernels in maize, resulting in light-colored kernels, demonstrating the mutagenic effect of transposition.
  • Transposons can cause mutations, alter gene expression, or lead to chromosomal rearrangements.

10. Overlapping Genes

Overlapping genes are defined as a pair (or more) of adjacent genes whose coding regions are partially or fully overlapping. This means that a single stretch of DNA codes for portions of two or more separate proteins.

  • This is a way for genomes, especially compact viral genomes, to encode more information within a limited amount of DNA.
  • The different proteins are typically read in different reading frames from the same DNA sequence.

11. Structural Genes

These genes contain the encoded information for the synthesis of chemical substances required for the cellular machinery and overall structure and function of the organism. Their products are directly involved in cellular activities.

The chemical substances may be:

  • Polypeptides for structural proteins: e.g., components of the protoplasm, cell membranes, elastin (ligaments), collagen (tendons, cartilage), actin (muscles), tubulin (microtubules).
  • Polypeptides for enzymes: Catalyzing biochemical reactions.
  • Transport proteins: e.g., hemoglobin (oxygen transport), lipid-transporting proteins, carrier proteins in cell membranes.
  • Proteinaceous hormones: e.g., insulin, growth hormone, parathyroid hormone.
  • Antibodies, antigens, certain toxins, blood coagulation factors.
  • Non-translated RNAs (functional RNAs): e.g., transfer RNAs (tRNAs), ribosomal RNAs (rRNAs).

Broadly speaking, structural genes either produce mRNAs that are translated into polypeptides/proteins/enzymes, or they produce non-coding RNAs that have direct functions.

12. Regulatory Genes (Regulatory Sequences)

Regulatory genes (or more accurately, regulatory sequences/elements) do not typically transcribe RNAs that directly contribute to the structure or functioning of cells in the same way structural genes do. Instead, they control the expression (activity) of other genes, particularly structural genes.

Important regulatory sequences include:

  • Promoters: DNA sequences where RNA polymerase binds to initiate transcription.
  • Terminators: DNA sequences that signal the end of transcription.
  • Operators: DNA sequences where repressor proteins bind to block transcription (common in prokaryotic operons).
  • Enhancers and Silencers: DNA sequences that can increase or decrease the transcription of genes, often located at a distance from the gene they regulate.

Some regulatory genes do produce proteins (e.g., repressor proteins, activator proteins, transcription factors). A gene that produces a repressor protein is of an intermediate nature: it's a structural gene for the repressor protein, but that protein then acts as a regulatory molecule.

13. Tissue Specific Genes

These are genes that are expressed only in certain specific tissues or cell types and not in others. Their selective expression is crucial for cell differentiation and the specialized functions of different tissues and organs.

  • Example: The gene for insulin is expressed primarily in the beta cells of the pancreas. The genes for muscle-specific proteins like myosin are expressed in muscle cells.

14. Junk DNA (Non-coding DNA)

This term, now considered somewhat outdated or misleading by many scientists, refers to DNA sequences within a genome that are not known to code for proteins or to have well-defined regulatory functions directly controlling gene expression.

  • Historically, large portions of eukaryotic genomes (up to 95% or more in humans) that didn't code for proteins were termed "junk DNA."
  • However, research is increasingly revealing that many of these non-coding regions have important roles, including:
    • Encoding functional non-coding RNAs (e.g., microRNAs, long non-coding RNAs) that regulate gene expression.
    • Containing regulatory elements like enhancers, silencers, insulators.
    • Playing roles in chromosome structure and organization.
    • Being involved in the evolution of new genes and possibly in DNA repair mechanisms.
  • While some DNA might truly be non-functional remnants of past evolutionary events (like ancient viral DNA integrations), the term "junk DNA" is less used now, with a preference for "non-coding DNA" to acknowledge its potential and often proven functionalities.

Gene Functions

Genes are the master architects and engineers of life, orchestrating a vast array of biological processes. Their functions are multifaceted and essential for the existence, continuity, and evolution of organisms.

  • Units of Inheritance: Genes are the fundamental components of genetic material, carrying hereditary information from one generation to the next.
  • Control of Phenotype: They determine the observable characteristics (morphology, physiology, behavior) of individuals.
  • Replication for Cell Division: The precise replication of genes is crucial for cell division (mitosis and meiosis), ensuring that daughter cells receive a complete set of genetic instructions.
  • Control of Metabolism and Structure: Genes direct the synthesis of proteins (including enzymes) that control the structure and metabolic activities of the body.
  • Generation of Variation: Reshuffling of genes during sexual reproduction (through processes like independent assortment and crossing over) produces genetic variation, which is the raw material for evolution.
  • Linkage and Recombination: Genes located on the same chromosome tend to be inherited together (linkage), but crossing over can create new combinations of alleles on a chromosome.
  • Mutation and Evolution: Genes can undergo mutations (changes in their DNA sequence), which can lead to altered traits. Mutations are a primary source of new genetic variation and are fundamental to evolution.
  • Formation of New Genes and Traits: Processes like exon shuffling (recombination of exons from different genes) and intron dynamics can contribute to the evolution of new genes and, consequently, new traits.
  • Modulation of Gene Expression: The expression of genes can be influenced by their position in the genome (position effect) and by the activity of transposable elements (transposons).
  • Cellular Differentiation: The development of different types of cells, tissues, and organs in a multicellular organism is controlled by the differential expression of genes – certain genes are activated while others are silenced in specific cell lineages.
  • Control of Development: The entire process of development, from a fertilized egg to a mature organism, including the various stages in the life history, is orchestrated by the regulated expression of genes.

Gene Structure Overview

Understanding the structure of a gene is key to understanding how it functions. While there are differences between prokaryotic and eukaryotic genes (discussed later), a general overview involves how genetic information flows from DNA to protein.

From DNA to Protein: The Central Dogma

The central dogma of molecular biology describes the two-step process, transcription and translation, by which the information in genes flows into proteins: DNA → RNA → Protein.

The diagram above illustrates the key steps in a eukaryotic cell:

  1. DNA within a Chromosome: Genes are segments of DNA located on chromosomes within the nucleus.
  2. Transcription: The DNA sequence of a gene is copied (transcribed) into a molecule of RNA, specifically messenger RNA (mRNA). In eukaryotes, this initial transcript is called pre-mRNA and includes both exons (coding regions) and introns (non-coding regions).
  3. RNA Processing (in Eukaryotes):
    • Capping: A modified guanine nucleotide (5' cap) is added to the 5' end of the pre-mRNA. This helps in mRNA export from the nucleus, protects it from degradation, and is involved in ribosome binding for translation.
    • Splicing: Introns are removed from the pre-mRNA, and the exons are joined together to form a continuous coding sequence.
    • Polyadenylation: A tail of adenine nucleotides (poly-A tail) is added to the 3' end. This also aids in export, stability, and translation initiation.
  4. Mature mRNA: After processing, the mature mRNA molecule moves from the nucleus to the cytoplasm through nuclear pores.
  5. Translation: In the cytoplasm, ribosomes bind to the mRNA and "read" its sequence of codons (three-nucleotide units). Each codon specifies a particular amino acid. Transfer RNA (tRNA) molecules bring the corresponding amino acids to the ribosome, and they are joined together to form a polypeptide chain, which then folds into a functional protein.

Exons and Introns: The Split Nature of Eukaryotic Genes

The diagram above further details the concept of split genes in eukaryotes:

  • DNA Level: The gene on the DNA contains both exons (coding regions, shown in color) and introns (non-coding regions, shown as lighter segments).
  • Transcription: The entire gene, including both exons and introns, is transcribed into a primary RNA transcript (pre-mRNA). A 5' cap and 3' poly-A tail are added.
  • Splicing: The introns are precisely cut out, and the exons are spliced (joined) together to form the mature mRNA transcript. This mature mRNA contains only the coding sequences that will be translated into protein.

Gene Organization: Prokaryotes vs. Eukaryotes

While the fundamental concept of a gene as a unit of heredity is universal, its structure and organization differ significantly between prokaryotic organisms (like bacteria and archaea) and eukaryotic organisms (like plants, animals, fungi, and protists).

Prokaryotic Gene Structure

Prokaryotic genes are generally simpler and more compact than eukaryotic genes. They are typically composed of three main regions:

  1. Promoter Region:
    • Located upstream (to the 5' side) of the RNA coding sequence.
    • This is the DNA sequence where RNA polymerase (the enzyme responsible for transcription) binds to initiate RNA synthesis.
    • The promoter provides the location and direction for transcription initiation.
    • Key consensus sequences (highly conserved sequences) in bacterial promoters include:
      • -10 sequence (Pribnow Box): Typically TATAAT, located about 10 base pairs upstream of the transcription start site.
      • -35 sequence: Typically TTGACA, located about 35 base pairs upstream.
    • These sequences are recognized by transcription factors (specifically the sigma factor subunit of RNA polymerase in bacteria).
  2. RNA Coding Sequence (Transcribed Region):
    • This is the actual DNA sequence that is copied (transcribed) into an RNA molecule (mRNA, tRNA, or rRNA).
    • It starts with an initiator codon (e.g., ATG in DNA, which becomes AUG in mRNA, often coding for methionine) and ends with a termination codon (e.g., TAA, TAG, TGA in DNA).
    • A crucial characteristic of prokaryotic protein-coding genes is that they are generally continuous and uninterrupted; they do not contain introns.
    • The DNA sequence is collinear with its mRNA product, meaning there's a direct correspondence between the nucleotide sequence in the DNA and the amino acid sequence in the protein (after accounting for the genetic code).
    • Nucleotides upstream of the transcription start site (+1) are denoted with negative numbers (e.g., -10, -35), while those downstream are positive.
  3. Terminator Region:
    • Located downstream (to the 3' side) of the RNA coding sequence.
    • This DNA sequence signals RNA polymerase to stop transcription.
    • Transcription termination in prokaryotes can occur through two main mechanisms:
      • Rho-dependent termination: Requires a protein factor called Rho.
      • Rho-independent termination (Intrinsic termination): Relies on specific sequences in the DNA/RNA that form a hairpin structure followed by a string of uracils in the RNA, causing RNA polymerase to dissociate.

The region 5' of the promoter is called the upstream sequence, and the region 3' of the terminator is the downstream sequence.

Eukaryotic Gene Structure

Eukaryotic genes are generally more complex in structure and regulation compared to prokaryotic genes. They are composed of several distinct regions:

  • Exons: The coding sequences that are transcribed and ultimately translated into amino acids in a polypeptide chain (or form functional RNAs). A gene starts and ends with exons. Some exons may include untranslated regions (UTRs) at the beginning or end of the mRNA. Exons vary in number, sequence, and length.
  • Introns: Non-coding sequences that are interspersed between exons. They are transcribed into the primary RNA transcript (pre-mRNA) but are then removed during RNA processing (splicing) before the mature mRNA is translated. All introns typically share conserved sequences at their boundaries: GT at the 5' end (splice donor site) and AG at the 3' end (splice acceptor site). Introns were first discovered in 1977 independently by Phillip Sharp and Richard Roberts.
  • Promoter Sequences: Regulatory regions of DNA located upstream (5') of the transcription start site, controlling gene expression. Eukaryotic promoters are more diverse and complex than prokaryotic ones.
    • Core Promoter: Contains the transcription start site (often around -34 bp relative to the start) and binding sites for general transcription factors and RNA polymerase (typically RNA Polymerase II for protein-coding genes). Common elements include the TATA box (similar to Pribnow box, but often around -25 to -30 bp).
    • Proximal Promoter: Contains primary regulatory elements located further upstream (e.g., -100 to -200 bp). These bind specific transcription factors that modulate the level and timing of transcription.
  • Terminator Sequences: Signal the end of transcription. Eukaryotic termination is often linked to polyadenylation signals.
  • Upstream Sequences (5' Flanking Region):
    • Includes the promoter and other regulatory elements.
    • The transcribed portion just before the start codon forms the 5' Untranslated Region (5'UTR) in the mRNA. The 5'UTR has roles in mRNA transport, stability, and initiation of translation.
    • Contains signals for the addition of the 5' cap (a modified 7-methylguanosine) to the mRNA. The cap facilitates translation initiation and stabilizes the mRNA.
  • Downstream Sequences (3' Flanking Region):
    • Includes sequences involved in transcription termination and polyadenylation.
    • The transcribed portion after the stop codon forms the 3' Untranslated Region (3'UTR) in the mRNA. The 3'UTR influences mRNA stability, localization, and translation efficiency. It contains the signal (e.g., AAUAAA) for the addition of the poly-A tail (a string of adenine nucleotides) to the 3' end of the mRNA. The poly-A tail also contributes to mRNA stability and translation.
    • The translation termination codon (e.g., TAA, TAG, TGA in DNA) is part of the last exon.
  • Enhancers and Silencers: Regulatory DNA sequences that can be located far upstream, downstream, or even within introns of a gene.
    • Enhancers: Bind activator proteins to increase the rate of transcription.
    • Silencers: Bind repressor proteins to decrease or shut down transcription.

Significance of Introns

While introns do not code for proteins, they are not merely "junk." They have several important cellular activities and evolutionary implications:

  • Regulatory Roles: Many introns encode small RNAs (e.g., microRNAs, snoRNAs) that are major regulators of gene expression. They can also contain regulatory sequences (like enhancers or silencers) that control transcription and mRNA processing.
  • Alternative Splicing: Introns allow for alternative splicing, a process where different combinations of exons from the same gene can be joined together. This enables a single gene to produce multiple different mRNA molecules and, consequently, multiple different proteins (isoforms) with potentially different functions. This greatly expands the coding capacity of the genome.
  • Evolutionary Role (Exon Shuffling): Introns can facilitate recombination between exons of different genes, a process called exon shuffling. This can lead to the creation of new genes with novel combinations of functional domains (encoded by exons), playing an important role in evolution.
  • Regulation of mRNA Export and Stability: The process of splicing itself can influence how efficiently mRNA is exported from the nucleus and its stability in the cytoplasm.
